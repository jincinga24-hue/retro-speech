## Part 1: Introduction (~2.5 min)

Hi everyone, my name is Aria — Zihan Ma. In this project with Proskill Workwear Australia, I've been working within the Research and AI Development function alongside Kim. Our core responsibility has been building the data infrastructure and predictive tools that power the team's sales and marketing strategy.

Before I get into the specifics, I want to share a bit about how I see my role within this team. Early on, when we did our team profiling exercise, I identified as a "Giver" — someone who naturally gravitates toward supporting teammates, sharing resources, and building a collaborative environment. That self-awareness has been important throughout this project because it shaped how I approached both my technical work and my interactions with the rest of the team.

In terms of strengths, I've always valued building strong relationships with team members and contributing to a positive, inclusive team environment. But I'm also aware of my growth areas — I tend to overcommit, which can risk burnout, and sometimes my sensitivity means I take feedback more personally than I should. One threat I identified early on was my tendency to leave tasks until later, which under pressure can lead to rushed work. Being conscious of these patterns has helped me manage them throughout this project — not perfectly, but more intentionally than before.

Within the Research and AI function, my key contributions have included helping build the structured prospect dataset — we compiled over 100 qualified leads across industries like civil construction, mining services, logistics, and steel fabrication. Each record includes the business name, industry, employee count, location, decision-maker contact, email, and LinkedIn profile. This dataset is what enables the predictive modelling and the automated outreach workflows that the team set as our core deliverables.

## Part 2: Decision Making & Conflict Resolution (~4 min)

### Decision Making

I want to highlight two key decisions our team navigated during this project.

**The first was around resource allocation — specifically, whether to prioritise upselling to Proskill's existing customers or focus on acquiring entirely new ones.** This came up early when we were scoping our approach. Chan and An, from the data analytics and marketing side, initially leaned toward existing customer retention because the data was more accessible and the conversion rates are typically higher. Kim and I, on the research side, saw a bigger opportunity in new customer acquisition because we believed our AI tools could identify high-potential prospects that Proskill hadn't reached yet.

Rather than forcing one direction, we brought it to a team discussion and agreed on a dual approach — we would do both, but with clear ownership. The marketing function would focus on existing customer strategies while research and AI would prioritise building the new prospect pipeline. The benefit of this decision was that it played to each sub-team's strengths instead of forcing everyone into the same lane. It also meant we didn't have to compromise on either goal — we could pursue both with dedicated focus.

**The second decision was about the AI modelling approach — specifically, which method to use for predicting when a customer might need new workwear.** Kim and I debated between a more complex machine learning model that could potentially deliver higher accuracy versus a simpler rule-based approach that we could actually build and validate within our project timeline. The complex model would have required more data than we had access to and more time to train and test.

We ultimately chose the simpler approach, and I think that was the right call. It gave us a working prototype we could actually demonstrate to the client and the team, rather than an ambitious model that might not have been ready in time. The key lesson here was about being pragmatic — the best solution isn't always the most technically sophisticated one; it's the one that delivers value within the constraints you're working with.

### Conflict Resolution

I also want to be transparent about a friction point we experienced, because I think it's important for the retrospective to be honest about challenges, not just successes.

**Midway through the project, there was a communication gap between the two sub-teams.** Kim and I had been building out the predictive dataset and some early AI workflow prototypes, but when we shared our outputs with Chan and An on the marketing side, they found it difficult to translate our work into actionable marketing campaigns. The data format wasn't quite right for their needs, some of the customer segments we defined didn't align with the categories they were using for outreach, and there wasn't enough context around why certain prospects were flagged as high-potential.

At first, this was frustrating on both sides. We felt like we'd done solid research work, and they felt like they couldn't use it. But instead of letting this become a blame situation, we set up a feedback loop. We scheduled a dedicated sync where the marketing team walked us through exactly what they needed — the format, the fields, the level of detail. And then Kim and I restructured our outputs to match those requirements.

The result was much better. The second version of our prospect dataset — the one with over 100 records — was built directly in response to that feedback. We included fields like industry classification, employee count ranges, and direct decision-maker contacts because the marketing side told us those were the variables that mattered most for outreach targeting.

The bigger takeaway for me was that **building something technically sound is not the same as building something useful.** The value of our work only materialised when it was usable by the people downstream. This experience taught me to involve stakeholders earlier and check alignment before building, not after.

## Part 3: Key Team Behaviours (~3.5 min)

Looking at our team's behaviours across this project, I want to highlight three patterns that I believe were critical to our progress.

### 1. Structured Coordination Across Functions

One behaviour that served us well was maintaining regular team meetings to review progress, validate key decisions, and adjust direction. This was especially important because our team was split into two distinct functions — data analytics and marketing on one side, research and AI development on the other. Without deliberate coordination, these two streams could easily have drifted apart.

For example, after the communication gap I just described, we committed to weekly syncs where each function would share not just what they'd done, but what they needed from the other side. This made our collaboration much more intentional rather than reactive. The benefit was that it kept research, data, and AI development aligned and feeding into each other, which is exactly what our team charter called for.

### 2. Playing to Individual Strengths

Another key behaviour was how we divided work based on individual expertise rather than splitting tasks evenly for the sake of fairness. Chan and An brought strong skills in data analytics and marketing strategy, so they led the customer segmentation, RFI identification, and campaign design. Kim and I focused on the research, data collection, and AI tool development.

This wasn't about creating silos — it was about maximising the quality of each workstream. When everyone works on what they're best at, the overall output is stronger. And because we had the coordination mechanisms in place, we avoided the silo risk. The result was that each function could go deep on their area while staying connected to the bigger picture.

### 3. Integrating Feedback Into Action

The third behaviour — and the one I'm most proud of as a team — is that we didn't just collect feedback; we acted on it. When the marketing team said our dataset wasn't usable, we restructured it. When we realised our AI approach was too ambitious, we scoped it down. When workload started to feel unbalanced, we talked about it openly.

This connects back to my role as a Giver. I naturally want to support others and make sure everyone's voice is heard. But what I learned through this project is that being a Giver isn't just about being supportive — it's about being responsive. It's about taking what the team gives you in terms of feedback and turning it into concrete changes.

As a team, I think we demonstrated strong collaboration by integrating all voices into shared actions and adaptations — which is ultimately what a retrospective is about. It's not just reflecting for the sake of reflecting; it's reflecting so we can do better next time.

### Closing

To wrap up, this project with Proskill Workwear taught me a lot — about the value of pragmatic decision-making, about the importance of building for your stakeholders and not just for technical correctness, and about how honest communication can turn friction into improvement. I'm grateful to Chan, An, and Kim for being a team that made it safe to be honest, and I look forward to carrying these lessons forward.

Thank you.
