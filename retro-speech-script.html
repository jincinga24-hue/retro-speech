2 Decision Making & Conflict Resolution ~4 min
Decision 1: Existing Customers vs. New Acquisition

The first major decision our team navigated was around resource allocation — whether to prioritise upselling to Proskill's existing customers or focus on acquiring new ones.

Chan and An, from the data analytics and marketing side, initially leaned toward existing customer retention because the data was more accessible and conversion rates are typically higher. Kim and I, on the research side, saw a bigger opportunity in new customer acquisition because we believed our AI tools could identify high-potential prospects that Proskill hadn't reached yet.

Credit a specific teammate idea here. For example:

"It was actually [Chan/An] who pointed out that [specific insight, e.g. existing customers had a 60% reorder rate / the mining sector had the highest repeat purchase volume], and that insight shaped how we split our approach."

Our solution: Rather than forcing one direction, we agreed on a dual approach with clear ownership. The marketing function would focus on existing customer strategies while research and AI would prioritise building the new prospect pipeline.

Why this was effective: It avoided a zero-sum trade-off. Instead of compromising on one goal, we gave each sub-team full ownership over their track. This meant faster execution because nobody was waiting on the other side to agree on priorities. It also reduced decision fatigue — once ownership was clear, each function could make day-to-day calls independently without needing whole-team consensus on every step.

What could be improved: In hindsight, we could have set clearer checkpoints to evaluate whether the dual approach was actually working, rather than just trusting that both tracks would converge. We also didn't define upfront how to handle overlap — for example, if a new prospect turned out to be an existing customer's subsidiary, which team owned that lead? Having a simple rule for edge cases would have saved some back-and-forth later.

Decision 2: AI Model Complexity vs. Feasibility

The second decision was about which AI approach to use for predicting when a customer might need new workwear. Kim and I debated between a more complex machine learning model that could deliver higher accuracy versus a simpler rule-based approach we could actually build and validate within our timeline.

Our solution: We chose the simpler rule-based approach — giving us a working prototype we could actually demonstrate and validate with the team and client, rather than an ambitious model that might not have been ready.

Why this was effective: A working prototype, even a simple one, gave the whole team something tangible to react to. Chan and An could test whether the predictions made sense from a marketing perspective, and we could get client feedback early. It also meant we weren't blocked — the marketing side could start designing campaigns around the outputs while Kim and I continued refining the logic. Delivering something usable on time was more valuable than delivering something impressive late.

What could be improved: We probably should have documented why we made this trade-off more formally. If someone picks up this project after us, they won't know whether we chose the simpler model because it was genuinely better or because we ran out of time. We also didn't build in a clear path to upgrade — ideally, we would have structured the simple model so it could be swapped out for a more complex one later without rebuilding the whole pipeline.

Conflict Resolution

I want to be honest here — our team didn't experience a major conflict during this project. And I think that's worth reflecting on, because it didn't happen by accident.

I believe the reason we avoided significant friction was because of the structures we put in place early. Dividing the team into two clear functions with defined ownership meant we weren't stepping on each other's toes. The weekly syncs gave us a regular space to raise concerns before they built up. And the fact that we explicitly discussed our working styles and roles at the beginning — through the team profiling exercise — meant we had a shared language for talking about how we work, not just what we work on.

Why I think this worked: Most conflict in teams doesn't come from big disagreements. It comes from small misalignments that go unspoken — unclear ownership, uneven workload, assumptions about what someone else is doing. By setting expectations early and checking in regularly, we addressed those small things before they had a chance to grow. The dual-track structure also helped because each function had autonomy. We didn't need to negotiate every decision as a group of four, which reduced the surface area for disagreement.

But I don't want to be naive about this either. There are risks I can see in how we worked that could have become conflicts if the project ran longer or the pressure was higher:

Risk 1: Work pacing. We all have different rhythms — some of us prefer to work ahead, others work closer to deadlines. In a short project this was manageable, but over a longer engagement, mismatched pacing could create resentment if one side feels the other isn't contributing at the same rate. If I were to do this again, I would set shared milestone dates earlier so the expectation is about "done by when," not "working at the same pace."
Risk 2: Visibility imbalance. Marketing outputs like campaign plans and outreach strategies are naturally more visible and easier to present than backend research and data work. Over time, this could make the research side feel undervalued. I'd want to be more intentional about showing how the data work directly enables the marketing outputs, so the contribution is visible even when the work itself isn't flashy.

What I'd improve for next time: I'd introduce a short "how are we working together?" check-in halfway through the project — separate from the task-focused syncs. Not because something is wrong, but because it creates a safe moment to surface small frustrations before they become real problems. Prevention is easier than repair.

3 Key Team Behaviours ~3.5 min
1. Structured Coordination Across Functions

One behaviour that served us well was maintaining regular team meetings to review progress, validate key decisions, and adjust direction. This was especially important because our team was split into two distinct functions — without deliberate coordination, these two streams could easily have drifted apart.

Example: We committed to weekly syncs where each function would share not just what they'd done, but what they needed from the other side. This made our collaboration intentional rather than reactive, and helped us catch misalignment early before it became a bigger issue.

The benefit was that it kept research, data, and AI development aligned and feeding into each other, which is exactly what our team charter called for.

2. Playing to Individual Strengths

Another key behaviour was dividing work based on individual expertise rather than splitting tasks evenly for the sake of fairness. Chan and An led the customer segmentation, RFI identification, and campaign design. Kim and I focused on research, data collection, and AI tool development.

Give 1-2 teammates specific credit. For example:

"Chan [did something specific, e.g. designed the outreach email templates / identified which industries to target first], and that directly shaped our campaign strategy."

"Kim [did something specific, e.g. found an open-source tool we used for data scraping / built the initial scoring model], which saved us significant time."

"An [did something specific, e.g. created the customer segmentation framework / mapped out the RFI submission process]."

Why this worked: When everyone works on what they're best at, the overall output is stronger. And because we had the coordination mechanisms in place, we avoided the silo risk. Each function could go deep on their area while staying connected to the bigger picture.
3. Integrating Feedback Into Action

The third behaviour — and the one I'm most proud of as a team — is that we didn't just collect feedback; we acted on it. When the marketing team said our dataset wasn't usable, we restructured it. When we realised our AI approach was too ambitious, we scoped it down. When workload started to feel unbalanced, we talked about it openly.

This connects back to my role as a Giver. I naturally want to support others and make sure everyone's voice is heard. But what I learned through this project is that being a Giver isn't just about being supportive — it's about being responsive. It's about taking what the team gives you in terms of feedback and turning it into concrete changes.
