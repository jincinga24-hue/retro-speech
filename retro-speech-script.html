Hi everyone, my name is Aria — Zihan Ma. In this project with Proskill Workwear Australia, I've been working within the Research and AI Development function alongside Kim. Each record includes the business name, industry, employee count, location, decision-maker contact, email, and LinkedIn profile. This dataset is what enables the predictive modelling and the automated outreach workflows that the team set as our core deliverables.

2 Decision Making & Conflict Resolution ~4 min
Decision 1: Existing Customers vs. New Acquisition

The first major decision our team navigated was around resource allocation — specifically, whether to prioritise upselling to Proskill's existing customers or focus on acquiring entirely new ones.

Chan and An, from the data analytics and marketing side, initially leaned toward existing customer retention because the data was more accessible and conversion rates are typically higher. Kim and I, on the research side, saw a bigger opportunity in new customer acquisition because we believed our AI tools could identify high-potential prospects that Proskill hadn't reached yet.

Credit a specific teammate idea here. For example:

"It was actually [Chan/An] who pointed out that [specific insight, e.g. existing customers had a 60% reorder rate / the mining sector had the highest repeat purchase volume], and that insight shaped how we split our approach."

What we did: Rather than forcing one direction, we brought it to a team discussion and agreed on a dual approach. The marketing function would focus on existing customer strategies while research and AI would prioritise building the new prospect pipeline.

The benefit of this decision was that it played to each sub-team's strengths instead of forcing everyone into the same lane. We could pursue both goals with dedicated focus rather than compromising on either.

Decision 2: AI Model Complexity vs. Feasibility

The second decision was about which AI approach to use for predicting when a customer might need new workwear. Kim and I debated between a more complex machine learning model that could deliver higher accuracy versus a simpler rule-based approach we could actually build and validate within our timeline.

The complex model would have required more data than we had access to and more time to train and test.

What we chose: The simpler approach — giving us a working prototype we could actually demonstrate, rather than an ambitious model that might not have been ready in time.

The key lesson here was about being pragmatic. The best solution isn't always the most technically sophisticated one; it's the one that delivers value within the constraints you're working with.

Conflict: The Communication Gap

I also want to be transparent about a friction point we experienced, because I think it's important for the retrospective to be honest about challenges, not just successes.

Midway through the project, there was a communication gap between the two sub-teams. Kim and I had been building out the predictive dataset and some early AI workflow prototypes, but when we shared our outputs with Chan and An on the marketing side, they found it difficult to translate our work into actionable marketing campaigns. The data format wasn't quite right, some customer segments didn't align with their outreach categories, and there wasn't enough context around why certain prospects were flagged as high-potential.

At first, this was frustrating on both sides. We felt we'd done solid research work, and they felt they couldn't use it.

How we resolved it: We set up a dedicated feedback loop. The marketing team walked us through exactly what they needed — the format, the fields, the level of detail. Kim and I then restructured our outputs to match those requirements. The second version of our prospect dataset — the one with over 100 records including industry, employee count ranges, and direct decision-maker contacts — was built directly in response to that feedback.

The bigger takeaway for me was that building something technically sound is not the same as building something useful. The value of our work only materialised when it was usable by the people downstream. This taught me to involve stakeholders earlier and check alignment before building, not after.

3 Key Team Behaviours ~3.5 min
1. Structured Coordination Across Functions

One behaviour that served us well was maintaining regular team meetings to review progress, validate key decisions, and adjust direction. This was especially important because our team was split into two distinct functions — without deliberate coordination, these two streams could easily have drifted apart.

Example: After the communication gap I described, we committed to weekly syncs where each function would share not just what they'd done, but what they needed from the other side. This made our collaboration intentional rather than reactive.

The benefit was that it kept research, data, and AI development aligned and feeding into each other, which is exactly what our team charter called for.

2. Playing to Individual Strengths

Another key behaviour was dividing work based on individual expertise rather than splitting tasks evenly for the sake of fairness. Chan and An led the customer segmentation, RFI identification, and campaign design. Kim and I focused on research, data collection, and AI tool development.

Give 1-2 teammates specific credit. For example:

"Kim [did something specific, e.g. found an open-source tool we used for data scraping / built the initial scoring model], which saved us significant time."

"An [did something specific, e.g. created the customer segmentation framework / mapped out the RFI submission process]."

Why this worked: When everyone works on what they're best at, the overall output is stronger. And because we had the coordination mechanisms in place, we avoided the silo risk. Each function could go deep on their area while staying connected to the bigger picture.
3. Integrating Feedback Into Action

The third behaviour — and the one I'm most proud of as a team — is that we didn't just collect feedback; we acted on it. When the marketing team said our dataset wasn't usable, we restructured it. When we realised our AI approach was too ambitious, we scoped it down. When workload started to feel unbalanced, we talked about it openly.

This connects back to my role as a Giver. I naturally want to support others and make sure everyone's voice is heard. But what I learned through this project is that being a Giver isn't just about being supportive — it's about being responsive. It's about taking what the team gives you in terms of feedback and turning it into concrete changes.
